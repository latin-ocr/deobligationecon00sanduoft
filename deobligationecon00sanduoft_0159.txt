8. Further: Even if the Law be unjust as to its Matter and Ob-

heavy tax, the Obligation is not destroyed necessarily. We must here
distinguish between what is merely unjust suffering, and what is
immoral doing. If the thing commanded be of the former kind,—-an
iniquitous infliction, not a wicked act in the sufferer,—the fault is in
him who gives the command. It is no crime to obey3 it is 3, fault
not to obey.

But if the thing commanded be morally bad, the doing it is a sin
in both parties,--him who commands, and him who obeyS.

And thus, the meaning of our Conclusion is, that Human Laws

which prohibit what is morally necessary, or command what is morally
unlawful, do not bind the Conscience.
